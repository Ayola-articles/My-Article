Section 29 - Does Section 29 compel the state to provide education?

Introduction

Section 29 is more than just a legal rule; it resembles our country’s promise to repair the deep scars left by apartheid. It stresses that every South African child should have the chance to overcome a history of inequality and build a future where opportunity and dignity are guaranteed.

Under apartheid, education was unequal. The Bantu Education Act ensured that Black South Africans received an inferior education, limiting their opportunities and increasing racial segregation. Schools for non-white learners were underfunded, poorly resourced, and made to prepare them for the unskilled sector, while white schools received better facilities and education. With democracy in 1994, Section 29 of the Constitution was enacted to undo this injustice, guaranteeing the right to education for all. This right is important for South Africa’s transformation, ensuring quality learning and promoting constitutional values and Ubuntu.

I am of the view that Section 29 of the Constitution compels the State to provide adequate protection for the unqualified right to education, giving effect to constitutional values and Ubuntu. In this essay, I will discuss arguments for and against the statement “Section 29 of the Constitution compels the State to provide adequate protection for the unqualified right to education, giving effect to constitutional values and Ubuntu.” I will refer to relevant case law and texts dealt with in the course. The issues to be discussed include the interpretation of Section 29, what it means to those relying on it, its limitations, and the embodiment of our constitutional values and Ubuntu in the educational sphere.

Understanding Section 29

In Section 29 of the Constitution, everyone has the right (a) to a basic education, including adult basic education; and (b) to further education, which the state, through reasonable measures, must make progressively available and accessible. Section 29(2) provides that everyone has the right to receive education in the official language or languages of their choice in public educational institutions where that education is reasonably practicable. To ensure effective access and implementation of this right, the state must consider all reasonable educational alternatives, including single-medium institutions, while taking into account equity, practicability, and the need to redress past discriminatory laws and practices.

Sections 29(3) and (4) provide that individuals may establish and maintain independent educational institutions at their own expense, provided they do not discriminate based on race, are registered with the state and maintain educational standards that are not inferior to those of public institutions. The state may also provide funding to private institutions, but this raises concerns about the allocation of public resources and equitable access to education.

The Transformative Role of Section 29

Section 29 was enacted as a foundation of post-democratic South Africa, ensuring that the right to education serves as a tool of transformation from past injustices, particularly the Bantu Education Act. The state’s obligation to provide a basic education is not limited to creating spaces in schools; it also extends to providing educational resources, including schools, classrooms, teachers, learning materials, and appropriate facilities for learners. The right to basic education is immediate and unqualified, meaning the state must ensure access to education without delay or conditions. In contrast, further education must be made progressively available and accessible, acknowledging the state's practical limitations, such as funding and infrastructure.

Furthermore, Section 36(1) of the Constitution allows for the limitation of rights, meaning that while the state is compelled to provide education, resource constraints may justify certain limitations, particularly regarding the immediate availability of further education.

Ubuntu and its Influence

Ubuntu originates from the Nguni expression umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu, which means that one's identity and humanity are shaped through relationships with others. Ubuntu champions togetherness within a community. This philosophy consists of values such as compassion, empathy, dignity, and mutual respect. It thrives on social harmony and collective well-being.

In South Africa's legal history, Ubuntu was referenced in the 1993 Interim Constitution's epilogue, which highlighted Ubuntu’s importance to the nation in its transition from apartheid to democracy. However, the 1996 Constitution does not mention Ubuntu, leading to debates on why it is omitted. Despite this absence, the Constitution contains values that are in unison with the principles of Ubuntu.

The Constitutional Court has played a pivotal role in integrating Ubuntu into South African jurisprudence. In cases like S v Makwanyane, the Court acknowledged Ubuntu as essential to the nation's constitutional values, influencing interpretations of justice and societal norms. Section 29 is a provision that reflects Ubuntu values by acknowledging education as a shared asset which is important for personal development and societal well-being.

Case Law

Moko v Acting Principal of Malusi Secondary School

The Constitutional Court's decision affirmed the right to education contained in Section 29 of the South African Constitution must be protected, drawing on both constitutional and Ubuntu values. The court's ruling delved into the state's duty to protect and fulfil the right to education, especially by ensuring that access to examinations is maintained as essential for academic progression, as shown by the order allowing Mr Moko to write his missed examination without delay. The Court addressed the violation of his educational rights, highlighting that education opens doors to transform one’s life, a notion that strongly correlates with Ubuntu’s views on social upliftment.

Moreover, the judgement acknowledged that educational authorities, such as the Acting Principal, have both negative and positive responsibilities under Section 29(1)(a) to protect and fulfil the right to education, which was a duty that was breached when Mr Moko was denied the opportunity to write his exam. Additionally, the decision reflects Ubuntu principles by recognising togetherness within the educational community and the collective duty to uphold each other’s rights and dignity. Although one might argue that the Acting Principal’s decision was reasonable on the grounds of Mr Moko not attending extra lessons, stressing individual accountability, the Court ultimately disapproved of this perspective by demonstrating that such an argument fails to consider the broader constitutional and social duty to ensure that no learner is deprived of educational opportunities.

Centre for Child Law and Others v Minister of Basic Education and Others

The 2019 judgement affirms the principle that all children, regardless of their documentation status, have the unqualified right to basic education. The Constitutional Court in this case reinforced the obligation of the State to ensure access to education for every child while championing both constitutional and Ubuntu values. The court ruled that excluding undocumented children from public schooling not only infringed on their constitutional right to basic education but also violated the principle of equality. This was evident in its finding that Circular 06 of 2016—which barred schools from receiving funding for undocumented learners—resulted in their unjust exclusion, thus contravening Section 29 of the South African Constitution.

The judgement further affirmed that all children possess inherent dignity and that their best interests must be paramount in any decision affecting them, a stance that aligns with Ubuntu principles, which emphasise the interconnectedness of humanity and the collective responsibility to care for all members of society. Although one might argue that documentation requirements are necessary to prevent fraudulent claims and ensure the fair allocation of public resources, the court rejected this view, holding that the admission policy is merely a policy rather than a law of general application and, as such, cannot be used to justify limitations on the constitutional right to education.

Opinion and Conclusion

In my opinion, Section 29 of the Constitution is a clear law that compels the State to ensure that every person who resides in South Africa, regardless of background, disability, age, or nationality, has immediate access to quality education. I am of the view that this unqualified right not only resembles our nation’s vision to heal the wounds of apartheid but also promotes the principle of social responsibility inherent in Ubuntu, which indicates that every person’s progress contributes to the collective upliftment of society.

Consequently, I believe that the State must allocate the necessary resources to ensure that all learners are guaranteed equal opportunities for education, thereby providing them with their inherent dignity and potential. Moreover, I argue that this approach is important for creating a culture of mutual respect, empathy, and unity, where education serves as a powerful force that lifts individuals and strengthens the bonds within our diverse country. Ultimately, I am convinced that protecting the unqualified right to education is the state’s legal obligation.

In conclusion, this essay has demonstrated that Section 29 of the Constitution obliges the State to provide immediate and quality basic education to everyone, irrespective of documentation status, as enshrined in the Constitution and reflected in Ubuntu values. The essay has shown how the right to basic education, as established in Section 29(1), demands immediate action to ensure every learner has access to the necessary resources and opportunities, while further education is to be progressively accessible under practical constraints. Moreover, the integration of Ubuntu principles—emphasising communal responsibility, mutual respect, and the inherent dignity of every individual—has been reflected in landmark judgements such as Moko v Acting Principal of Malusi Secondary School and Centre for Child Law and Others v Minister of Basic Education and Others. These cases underscore the transformative role of education in healing past injustices and fostering an inclusive society, while also reinforcing the State’s duty to safeguard the rights of all citizens. Ultimately, I maintain that ensuring the unqualified right to education is not only a constitutional mandate but also a moral imperative that supports the collective progress of our nation, and future policies must continue to reflect these values.
